## Abstract

This paper presents Phase 1 of the Reachy EQ/PPE Degree System (REPDS), a privacy-first emotion classification pipeline designed for the Reachy Mini social robot. Phase 1 is deliberately scoped to the offline machine learning problem: (i) construction of a human-in-the-loop synthetic data curation pipeline, (ii) training and model selection between two head-to-head fine-tuning variants of an EfficientNet-B0 backbone pre-trained on VGGFace2 and AffectNet, and (iii) a statistically principled comparison that nominates a single model for downstream deployment. All raw video is processed on a three-node local area network; only non-biometric JSON events traverse the network boundary. The training corpus consists of 11,911 synthetic videos generated by Luma AI Dream Machine (3,589 happy, 5,015 sad, 3,307 neutral), from which 86,519 face-cropped frames are used for training and 28,840 for held-out validation. A real-world generalization set of 894 AffectNet images provides an independent deployment-tier test.

Two candidate fine-tuning strategies are defined, each targeting the same HSEmotion `enet_b0_8_best_vgaf` backbone. **Variant 1 (V1)** freezes the pre-trained backbone and trains only a new 3-class linear head on face-cropped synthetic frames. **Variant 2 (V2)** initializes from the Variant 1 checkpoint, then selectively unfreezes the top two inverted-residual blocks (`blocks.5`, `blocks.6`) plus the `conv_head`, applying a differential learning-rate schedule (head 1├Ś10Ōü╗┬│, backbone 1├Ś10Ōü╗Ōü┤), label smoothing (╬Ą = 0.10), dropout (p = 0.3), Mixup (╬▒ = 0.2), cosine annealing with a 3-epoch warmup, and FP16 mixed precision. Best-epoch selection is governed by a two-tier Gate A policy: *Gate A-val* on synthetic validation (F1 Ōēź 0.84, balanced accuracy Ōēź 0.85, per-class F1 Ōēź 0.75, ECE Ōēż 0.12, Brier Ōēż 0.16) and *Gate A-deploy* on real-world test data (F1 Ōēź 0.75). Hyperparameter optimization for V2 is managed by a two-stage sweep comprising 96 stage-1 trials and 5 stage-2 promoted trials across `{lr, dropout, label_smoothing, freeze_epochs, mixup_alpha}`, tracked by MLflow and ranked on a composite gate-aware score.

The statistical framework implemented in the project's `stats/scripts/` module follows a four-stage decision tree: (1) two-tier Gate A quality validation, (2) McNemar's test on paired accuracy, (3) StuartŌĆōMaxwell marginal-homogeneity test on the 3-class confusion structure, and (4) per-class proportion comparisons with BenjaminiŌĆōHochberg false discovery rate correction. Because the held-out test set is shared between the two models, McNemar's test and the StuartŌĆōMaxwell test apply directly to the paired predictions.

On the synthetic validation tier, Variant 2 passes Gate A-val with F1_macro = 0.9994, balanced accuracy = 0.9994, ECE = 0.0796, and Brier = 0.0109, while Variant 1 narrowly fails Gate A-val due to ECE = 0.1242 at its best-F1 epoch. ## 1. Motivation and Background

### 1.1 Project Significance

The capacity for machines to perceive, interpret, and respond to human emotional states is one of the most consequential frontiers of applied artificial intelligence. As social robots become embedded in healthcare, education, eldercare, and therapeutic environments, their usefulness depends not merely on functional task completion but on *emotional attunement* ŌĆö the ability to recognize when a person is anxious, joyful, fatigued, or distressed, and to modulate response accordingly. The Reachy EQ/PPE Degree System (REPDS) addresses this challenge by constructing a locally deployable, privacy-preserving pipeline that classifies emotional states from video-based observation and nominates a single model for downstream deployment on the Reachy Mini robot (Pollen Robotics, 2025).

Phase 1 of REPDS is deliberately circumscribed. It covers only the offline machine learning problem: (i) synthetic-data generation and human-in-the-loop curation, (ii) supervised training of a small family of candidate classifiers on those curated frames, and (iii) a statistically disciplined comparison that nominates *one* model for Jetson export. It does *not* cover Phase 2 (degree-of-emotion modeling, calibration-driven gesture modulation, and LLM prompt tailoring) or Phase 3 (TensorRT export, DeepStream integration, and Jetson shadow rollout); those phases are deferred and reference only the Phase-1 nominated checkpoint as their starting artifact.

Even with this restricted scope, Phase 1 is significant for several reasons. First, it operates at the intersection of three rapidly maturing but rarely integrated research domains: affective computing (Picard, 1997), transfer learning for facial emotion recognition (Savchenko, 2022; Pan & Yang, 2010), and principled statistical comparison of classifier variants (Raschka, 2018; Solanas et al., 2024). Second, by constraining all raw video processing to a local area network (LAN) and transmitting only JSON-serialized metadata downstream, REPDS addresses a major barrier to adoption in sensitive environments ŌĆö namely, the transmission of biometric data to cloud services subject to corporate data policies and potential security breaches. Third, the project employs a principled human-in-the-loop curation pipeline in which an end user continuously validates and annotates synthetic video data, enabling iterative model improvement without requiring large pre-labeled corpora of real human subjects. Fourth, the project formalizes model selection as a statistical decision procedure rather than a leaderboard sort, which is consistent with best-practice recommendations from the applied ML experimentation literature (Michelucci & Venturini, 2025).

### 1.2 Personal and Institutional Motivation

This investigation is motivated by the researcher's background at the intersection of linguistics, business administration, and data science, with particular specialization in agentic AI, embodied systems, and machine learning operations (MLOps). The Reachy Mini platform ŌĆö a small, open-source social robot developed by Pollen Robotics ŌĆö presents an ideal testbed for a system that must reconcile the computational constraints of edge deployment with the real-time demands of humanŌĆōrobot interaction. The project is further motivated by the observation that most published emotion recognition systems rely on large cloud-hosted model inference pipelines that are impractical for deployment in privacy-sensitive contexts such as clinical assessment, child education, or workplace well-being monitoring.

The institutional motivation stems from DS 796 requirements to demonstrate comprehensive data science research competence: motivating a problem, designing an experiment, executing a machine learning pipeline, performing rigorous statistical analysis, and critically evaluating results. REPDS Phase 1 satisfies each of these dimensions while producing a functional, deployable artifact with genuine research value. The decision to scope the paper narrowly to Phase 1 reflects the pedagogical principle that a single, thoroughly executed experiment is more valuable than a shallow survey of an entire system (Michelucci & Venturini, 2025).

### 1.3 Stakeholders and Beneficiaries

The primary beneficiaries of REPDS fall into several overlapping groups. In clinical and therapeutic settings, social robots capable of detecting emotional state could support practitioners monitoring patient affect without requiring continuous human observation (Abdi et al., 2018). In educational contexts ŌĆö particularly language learning and social-skills development ŌĆö an emotionally responsive robot tutor offers more naturalistic and adaptive interaction than a static application (Leite et al., 2013). In eldercare, a Reachy-class robot capable of recognizing loneliness, confusion, or distress could serve as both companion and early-warning system. For researchers in affective computing, the open architecture of REPDS ŌĆö built on publicly available tools including HSEmotion (Savchenko, 2022), PyTorch, MLflow, and n8n ŌĆö provides a reproducible reference implementation for edge-deployed emotion recognition.

Secondary beneficiaries include robotics engineers working on the Reachy Mini ecosystem, data scientists exploring synthetic-data training regimes (Nikolenko, 2021), and policymakers and ethicists concerned with privacy-preserving AI standards. The project's local-first architecture directly implements the principle that sensitive perceptual data need not leave a controlled environment to support intelligent computation.

### 1.4 Challenges and Broader Implications

The principal challenges of Phase 1 involve four areas. First, the quality and representational adequacy of synthetic video generated by Luma AI Dream Machine as a substitute for real human emotion data. Generative video models are subject to demographic and expression biases that are not fully characterized in the research literature, and these biases propagate into the curated dataset. Second, the construction of a statistically rigorous evaluation framework capable of detecting genuine performance differences between V1 and V2 while controlling false discovery rate across multiple per-class comparisons (Benjamini & Hochberg, 1995). Third, the two-tier nature of Gate A, which requires the same evaluation pipeline to apply distinct pass/fail thresholds depending on whether the evaluation set is synthetic (`Gate A-val`, F1 Ōēź 0.84) or real-world (`Gate A-deploy`, F1 Ōēź 0.75). Fourth, the tension between aggregate statistical superiority and deployment-safe error profiles: a model that wins on macro-F1 may nonetheless be rejected for deployment because its residual errors are *structured* in ways that would produce inappropriate downstream behavior.

The broader implications are significant. If synthetic-data-trained models can satisfy a rigorous two-tier gate on a real-world test set, this result validates a privacy-preserving training paradigm that avoids the ethical and legal complications of collecting facial video data from human subjects. Moreover, the explicit separation of *statistical best* from *deployment-safe* under an asymmetric error-cost model suggests a template for deploying production-grade AI systems in human-facing, resource-constrained, network-isolated environments ŌĆö a class of deployment context that is growing as AI diffuses into homes, schools, and community facilities.

## 2. Research Platform and Managing the Data Science Analysis

### 2.1 Overall Plan of Investigation

The Phase 1 investigation proceeds along a sequential plan that mirrors the project's `stats/` and `trainer/` module layout. First, synthetic emotion videos are generated on Ubuntu 2 (10.0.4.140) via the Luma AI Dream Machine text-to-video API using a structured prompt strategy that controls emotion category, demographic variation, environmental context, and emotion intensity. Generated clips land in `/media/project_data/reachy_emotion/videos/temp/`, where the researcher uses a Streamlit curation UI (`apps/web/pages/02_Label.py`) to accept, relabel, or discard each clip. Accepted clips are promoted into `videos/train/<label>/` via the canonical `POST /api/v1/media/promote` endpoint, with the legacy `/api/v1/promote/stage` shim deprecated per `AGENTS.md` ┬¦3.

Second, once per-class counts cross a 1:1:1 balance threshold, a training run is triggered from the web UI's `03_Train.py` page or by the n8n Training Orchestrator agent. The training script (`trainer/train_variant1.py` for V1 or `trainer/train_variant2.py` for V2) invokes the `DatasetPreparer` class (`trainer/prepare_dataset.py`), which performs frame extraction (10 frames per source video, stratified random sampling), OpenCV DNN SSD face cropping, and a 75/25 train/validation split at the clip level. The resulting run-scoped frame datasets are stored under `videos/train/run/<run_id>/{happy,sad,neutral}/` and `videos/validation/run/<run_id>/{happy,sad,neutral}/`. Training parameters are logged to MLflow with full lineage, including dataset hash and run manifest.

Third, the trained best-epoch checkpoint is evaluated against two tiers. The synthetic validation tier (*Gate A-val*) uses the 25% held-out split from the same synthetic corpus, and the real-world tier (*Gate A-deploy*) uses a stratified 894-image subset of AffectNet (Mollahosseini et al., 2019) prepared via `trainer/ingest_affectnet.py` and `trainer/create_affectnet_datasets.py`. Gate results are written to `stats/results/runs/train/<run_id>.json` and `stats/results/runs/test/<run_id>.json` in a standardized JSON schema that captures both metrics and pass/fail per gate.

Fourth, the statistical comparison pipeline (`stats/scripts/`) is invoked to produce a decision for promoting one model to deployment. The pipeline computes quality-gate reports (`01_quality_gate_metrics.py`), StuartŌĆōMaxwell marginal-homogeneity analysis (`02_stuart_maxwell_test.py`), per-class paired analysis with BenjaminiŌĆōHochberg FDR correction (`03_perclass_paired_ttests.py`), and the integrated end-to-end runner (`04_phase1_statistical_pipeline.py`). A deployment recommendation is recorded in `stats/results/runs/analysis_run_<run_id>.md`.

### 2.3 Software Development and New Technologies

The Phase 1 development effort spans several interdisciplinary competencies. On the ML side, the researcher implemented and validated two sibling training entry points: `trainer/train_variant1.py` for the frozen-backbone head-only baseline, and `trainer/train_variant2.py` for the selectively unfrozen fine-tuning successor. Both scripts wrap the shared `EfficientNetTrainer` class (`trainer/fer_finetune/train_efficientnet.py`), which implements two-phase training (frozen warmup followed by selective unfreezing), mixed-precision via `torch.cuda.amp`, Mixup (Zhang et al., 2018, referenced in the augmentation pipeline), cosine annealing with warmup, gradient clipping, and early stopping on `val_f1_macro`. The HSEmotion backbone is loaded through the `model_efficientnet.py` module, which normalizes the `enet_b0_8_best_vgaf` weights onto the `timm`-style EfficientNet-B0 architecture and replaces the 8-class head with a new 3-class linear layer.

Face cropping was introduced in run_0107 and was the single largest empirical improvement in the project's history (see ┬¦4.7). Prior to run_0107, the training pipeline operated on full frames, which produced high synthetic validation accuracy but catastrophic real-world test accuracy (F1 Ōēł 0.43). After face cropping, real-world F1 jumped to approximately 0.78 for both variants ŌĆö an absolute gain of +0.35 on AffectNet. This finding is consistent with the emotion-recognition literature's emphasis on face-normalized inputs for expression classifiers (Savchenko, 2022; Mollahosseini et al., 2019).

The MLflow integration module (`trainer/mlflow_tracker.py`) logs all hyperparameters, per-epoch metrics, confusion matrices, and model artifacts to a centralized experiment server, enabling systematic comparison across training runs. The `trainer/save_run_artifacts.py` module standardizes the JSON schema written to `stats/results/runs/` so that downstream dashboards and the statistical pipeline can consume run data without custom parsing.

The statistical analysis pipeline ŌĆö located in the `stats/scripts/` directory ŌĆö implements four stages: (1) quality-gate assessment via `01_quality_gate_metrics.py` using macro F1, per-class F1, balanced accuracy, ECE, and Brier; (2) StuartŌĆōMaxwell marginal-homogeneity testing via `02_stuart_maxwell_test.py` on the paired 3-class confusion between V1 and V2; (3) per-class paired comparison via `03_perclass_paired_ttests.py` with BenjaminiŌĆōHochberg FDR correction; and (4) the integrated runner `04_phase1_statistical_pipeline.py`, which consumes prediction `.npz` artifacts from both variants and produces a single JSON report. These scripts are executed from the command line against stored prediction arrays and integrated into the n8n Evaluation Agent workflow.

The n8n orchestration layer required substantial workflow design. Ten cooperating agents were specified (Ingest, Labeling, Promotion, Reconciler, Training Orchestrator, Evaluation, Deployment, Privacy, Observability, and the Phase 3 Reachy Gesture Agent; see `AGENTS.md`). Phase 1 exercises agents 1ŌĆō6 in production; agents 7ŌĆō9 operate in passive/audit mode; agent 10 is deferred. The first three agents (Ingest, Labeling, Promotion) are 100% production-ready per the requirements changelog (`memory-bank/requirements.md` ┬¦20).

New technologies acquired for Phase 1 include: (a) `emotiefflib` / HSEmotion pretrained weight loading for emotion-specific transfer learning, (b) the `DatasetPreparer` run-scoped frame extraction convention with per-run validation mirrors, (c) the two-tier Gate A policy formalized in ADR 011 (`memory-bank/decisions/011-two-tier-gate-a-v1-deployment.md`), (d) the BenjaminiŌĆōHochberg procedure applied via `statsmodels.stats.multitest.multipletests`, and (e) the MLflow-centric composite-score leaderboard used by `stats/results/sweep/leaderboard.json` to rank V2 sweep trials.

### 2.4 Unexpected Challenges

Several unexpected challenges arose during Phase 1 execution. The most consequential ŌĆö documented in `stats/results/runs/analysis_run_0107.md` ŌĆö was the discovery that pre-run_0107 training pipelines operated on *full frames* rather than face-cropped frames. Synthetic validation scores at the time were near-perfect (F1 > 0.98), but real-world test scores were close to chance (F1 Ōēł 0.43). The introduction of OpenCV DNN SSD face cropping in run_0107 nearly doubled real-world F1 and reduced ECE by approximately 37 percent, resolving what had previously been labeled an intractable synthetic-to-real generalization gap. This experience confirmed Michelucci and Venturini's (2025) admonition that domain alignment of preprocessing is often more impactful than architectural choice.

A second challenge involved calibration behavior (Expected Calibration Error). Early Variant 2 configurations failed Gate A-val on ECE despite exceeding all other metrics. Sweeping label-smoothing ╬Ą in `{0.05, 0.10, 0.15}` and dropout in `{0.3, 0.4, 0.5}` revealed that ╬Ą = 0.10 paired with dropout = 0.3 produced a V2 configuration (`var2_sweep_s2_t004`) that cleared all Gate A-val thresholds simultaneously. This result is consistent with M├╝ller et al.'s (2019) finding that modest label smoothing improves calibration without harming classification accuracy.

A third challenge was dataset balance. The initial Luma AI generation sweep produced more happy and neutral clips than sad clips, creating a class imbalance that inflated macro accuracy while suppressing per-class F1 for the sad class. This motivated the explicit adoption of balanced accuracy as a co-primary evaluation metric alongside macro F1 and the implementation of stratified k-fold cross-validation at the *clip* level to preserve class proportion across training and validation splits while preventing temporal leakage within multi-frame clips.

A fourth challenge, specific to the statistical-analysis pipeline, was the decision of which marginal-homogeneity test to apply. The project originally planned to use Stuart's (1955) classical test, but the 3-class paired table produced by the McNemar-style aggregation is sparse in the off-diagonal cells because the AffectNet real-world test set contains only 894 samples stratified over three emotion categories. Bhapkar's (1966) extension is preferred for small sparse tables, and the current implementation in `02_stuart_maxwell_test.py` includes both statistics with a fallback.

## 3. Literature Review

### 3.1 Affective Computing and Emotion Recognition

The field of affective computing was formally established by Picard (1997), who argued that machines capable of recognizing, interpreting, and simulating human emotional states would achieve fundamentally richer and more useful interactions with human users. Two theoretical frameworks have dominated computational emotion representation. The *discrete emotion model*, developed most influentially by Ekman (1992), proposes a set of six basic emotions ŌĆö happiness, sadness, anger, fear, disgust, and surprise ŌĆö argued to be biologically universal and recognizable across cultures from facial muscle configurations described by the Facial Action Coding System (FACS). The *dimensional model*, advanced by Russell (1980), conceptualizes emotions as points in a continuous two-dimensional space defined by valence (positiveŌĆōnegative) and arousal (highŌĆōlow). REPDS Phase 1 uses a deliberately minimal 3-class slice of Ekman's taxonomy (`happy`, `sad`, `neutral`) to reduce the combinatorial cost of data curation while preserving the most psychologically distinct emotion triad for a companion robot. The reserved classes (`anger`, `surprise`, `fear`, `disgust`, `contempt`) are documented in `memory-bank/requirements.md` ┬¦19 as Phase 2+ targets.

Facial expression recognition (FER) has emerged as the dominant perceptual modality for automated emotion classification, benefiting from the accessibility of RGB video capture and the availability of large annotated datasets. Benchmark datasets for FER research include AffectNet (Mollahosseini et al., 2019), containing approximately one million manually annotated facial images; RAF-DB (Li et al., 2017), a real-world affective face database; and the Extended CohnŌĆōKanade dataset (CK+; Lucey et al., 2010), a laboratory-controlled sequence dataset. REPDS Phase 1 departs from these benchmark paradigms in one crucial way: training data is generated exclusively by Luma AI Dream Machine, not collected from real human subjects. AffectNet is used only as a real-world test set, never as training data. This design choice is motivated by privacy and ethical considerations ŌĆö no real human subjects are required for training data collection, and no identifiable facial biometrics are stored or transmitted across the LAN boundary.

The application of emotion-aware systems to social robotics has been explored in a growing literature. Breazeal (2003) demonstrated with the Kismet robot that affective cues ŌĆö facial expression, vocal tone, gesture ŌĆö could be integrated into a robot architecture to produce natural, engaging humanŌĆōrobot interaction. More recently, systems integrating deep-learning-based emotion recognition with robot control have shown promising results in eldercare (Abdi et al., 2018), autism therapy (Scassellati et al., 2012), and educational tutoring (Leite et al., 2013). REPDS contributes to this line of research by providing an open, reproducible, privacy-preserving architecture suitable for resource-constrained social robots, and by formalizing model selection as a statistical decision procedure rather than a leaderboard sort.

### 3.2 Deep Learning for Facial Emotion Recognition

The transformation of facial emotion recognition from hand-crafted feature pipelines to end-to-end deep learning representations followed the broader trajectory of computer vision, accelerating after the introduction of large-scale convolutional neural networks (LeCun, Bengio & Hinton, 2015). Key architectural advances include the introduction of very deep networks with residual connections (He et al., 2016), which addressed the vanishing gradient problem and enabled training of networks exceeding 50 layers; and EfficientNet (Tan & Le, 2019), which introduced a compound scaling strategy balancing network depth, width, and resolution to achieve state-of-the-art accuracy at reduced computational cost. The EfficientNet-B0 model used in REPDS has 5.3M parameters ŌĆö approximately one-fifth the parameter count of ResNet-50 ŌĆö and achieves comparable or better accuracy on ImageNet while fitting comfortably within the 8 GB unified memory envelope of the Jetson Xavier NX.

For emotion-specific applications, the HSEmotion library (Savchenko, 2022) provides pretrained EfficientNet-B0 and EfficientNet-B2 models fine-tuned on VGGFace2 and AffectNet, representing a mature starting point for transfer learning in FER tasks. The specific checkpoint used by REPDS, `enet_b0_8_best_vgaf`, is an EfficientNet-B0 backbone trained for 8-class emotion classification on the VGAF (VGGFace2 + AffectNet) composite dataset. Because the pretraining domain (facial emotion recognition) is closely aligned with the REPDS target task (3-class emotion classification), the domain gap that a model must close during fine-tuning is much smaller than if ImageNet-only initialization were used. This observation is consistent with Pan and Yang's (2010) argument that transfer learning is most effective when source and target domains share both input modality and task structure.

Temporal modeling is relevant when emotion is classified from video rather than static frames. Architectures combining spatial CNN feature extraction with temporal aggregation ŌĆö including 3D CNNs (Tran et al., 2015), temporal segment networks (Wang et al., 2016), and transformer-based temporal attention (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) ŌĆö offer potential accuracy advantages over frame-level classification averaged across clip segments. REPDS Phase 1 employs frame-level sampling (10 random frames per five-second clip) averaged at inference time, a computationally tractable approach that sacrifices temporal structure for Jetson Xavier NX compatibility. Future phases may incorporate lightweight temporal aggregation to improve recognition of emotion trajectories, but Phase 1 does not attempt this.

### 3.3 Transfer Learning and Domain Adaptation

Transfer learning, the practice of initializing a model with weights learned on one task or domain and adapting them to a related target task, has become a foundational technique in applied deep learning (Pan & Yang, 2010). The standard transfer learning workflow for image classification involves two stages: (1) feature extraction, in which all pretrained convolutional layers are frozen and only a new classification head is trained; and (2) fine-tuning, in which the upper convolutional blocks are unfrozen and jointly updated with the head at a reduced learning rate (PyTorch Tutorials, 2024). The PyTorch transfer learning tutorial explicitly describes these two scenarios as complementary strategies appropriate for different dataset size and similarity conditions. REPDS operationalizes this two-stage taxonomy as the V1 (feature-extraction) vs. V2 (fine-tuning) contrast that is the central experiment of this paper.

Michelucci and Venturini (2025) provide comprehensive best-practice guidance for ML experimentation in scientific contexts, recommending that researchers (i) establish simple baseline models before attempting complex architectures, (ii) employ k-fold cross-validation or its leak-free equivalents to estimate metric variance without overfitting test data, (iii) apply regularization techniques including dropout and weight decay alongside early stopping to prevent overfitting during fine-tuning, and (iv) separate model selection (hyperparameter tuning) from final performance estimation via held-out test sets or nested cross-validation. These recommendations directly inform the REPDS experimental design: V1 serves as the simple feature-extraction baseline, V2 is produced only by applying fine-tuning to the V1 checkpoint, the DatasetPreparer class enforces 75/25 stratified clip-level splits, and the AffectNet real-world test set is never seen by the sweep loop.

The particular challenge of domain adaptation in REPDS arises from the *synthetic-to-real* distribution gap: models trained on Luma AI-generated video must generalize to real facial expressions at inference time. Research on synthetic-to-real transfer (Nikolenko, 2021) suggests that careful synthetic data diversity, including variation in lighting conditions, facial geometry, expression intensity, and background environment, is critical to reducing this gap. The Luma AI generation prompt strategy was designed to maximize this diversity, generating clips across simulated demographics, lighting environments, and emotion intensities. Face cropping as a preprocessing step was shown empirically in run_0107 to dramatically close this gap ŌĆö confirming the preprocessing-first principle emphasized in Michelucci and Venturini (2025).

### 3.4 Edge AI Inference and Deployment

The deployment of deep neural networks on resource-constrained edge hardware is a rapidly maturing research and engineering discipline. The NVIDIA Jetson platform family has emerged as a leading edge AI deployment target, combining GPU acceleration with a low-power envelope suitable for embedded and mobile applications. TensorRT, NVIDIA's inference optimization library (NVIDIA, 2023), reduces model latency and memory footprint through layer fusion, quantization (INT8, FP16), and kernel auto-tuning. DeepStream SDK provides a GStreamer-based pipeline framework for multi-stream video analytics, enabling efficient batched inference from camera inputs with configurable pre- and post-processing stages.

The quality gate thresholds specified for REPDS ŌĆö latency p50 Ōēż 120 ms, p95 Ōēż 250 ms, GPU memory Ōēż 2.5 GB ŌĆö are grounded in the Jetson Xavier NX's 8 GB unified memory architecture and the real-time interaction requirements of social robotics. These constraints require FP16 mixed-precision inference rather than full FP32, a standard practice in edge deployment that sacrifices minimal accuracy (typically < 0.5 percent on classification benchmarks) for substantial memory and latency reductions (Li et al., 2020). Phase 1 does *not* perform formal edge-deployment validation (that is the scope of Phase 3), but the project's model selection is constrained such that the nominated checkpoint must be convertible to FP16 TensorRT without structural incompatibilities. EfficientNet-B0 was chosen in part because its inverted-residual block structure has been verified to convert cleanly to TensorRT under the TAO 5.3 export pipeline.

### 3.5 Agentic AI Orchestration

The concept of *agentic AI* ŌĆö AI systems comprising collections of autonomous agents capable of goal-directed action, tool use, and inter-agent communication ŌĆö has moved from theoretical proposal to practical implementation over the past three years, driven largely by advances in large language model capability and the development of agent framework infrastructure (Wang et al., 2024). Prominent orchestration frameworks include LangChain, AutoGen (Wu et al., 2023), CrewAI, and n8n. REPDS employs n8n as its orchestration layer, chosen for its visual workflow editor, webhook-based event triggering, and native integration with PostgreSQL, REST APIs, and shell commands ŌĆö capabilities that map directly onto the ten-step dataset curation, model training, evaluation, and deployment lifecycle documented in `AGENTS.md`.

The ten-agent architecture of REPDS represents a division of labor across three functional clusters: data management agents (Ingest, Labeling, Promotion, Reconciler), training agents (Training Orchestrator, Evaluation), and deployment/operations agents (Deployment, Privacy, Observability, Reachy Gesture). This design follows the principle of single-responsibility in agent architecture (Wang et al., 2024), wherein each agent is specialized for one pipeline function, improving debuggability and enabling independent scaling or substitution of individual components. For Phase 1, only agents 1ŌĆō6 are exercised as first-class participants; agents 7ŌĆō10 operate in audit or simulated-only modes.

### 3.6 Statistical Evaluation of Machine Learning Models

Rigorous statistical comparison of machine learning model variants is an area where practice has often lagged behind recommendation. Solanas et al. (2024) provide a comprehensive survey of evaluation metrics and statistical tests appropriate for binary and multi-class classification tasks, noting that *paired* tests are required when the same test instances are evaluated by both models being compared, and that nonparametric tests should be preferred when metric distributions across cross-validation folds cannot be assumed normal. Their analysis supports the use of Wilcoxon signed-rank tests as a more robust alternative to paired t-tests for fold-level metric comparison.

For pairwise comparison of classifier accuracy on a shared test set, McNemar's test (McNemar, 1947) is the appropriate method (Raschka, 2018). McNemar's test evaluates whether two classifiers differ in the frequency of their disagreements ŌĆö specifically, whether the number of instances classified correctly by model A but not B differs significantly from the number classified correctly by B but not A. The `mlxtend` library (Raschka, 2018) provides a well-validated Python implementation used in REPDS statistical analysis. For cases involving multiple simultaneous comparisons ŌĆö such as per-class F1 score comparisons across multiple emotion categories ŌĆö the BenjaminiŌĆōHochberg procedure for false discovery rate control (Benjamini & Hochberg, 1995) provides a less conservative and more powerful correction than the Bonferroni method.

The StuartŌĆōMaxwell test (Stuart, 1955; Maxwell, 1970) extends McNemar's test to multinomial classification problems, testing whether the marginal distributions of predicted class labels differ significantly between two classifiers. This test is particularly appropriate for REPDS because the 3-class (and eventually 8-class) emotion taxonomy renders simple McNemar analysis insufficient for distinguishing *where* the two classifiers differ. The `stats/scripts/02_stuart_maxwell_test.py` script implements the marginal homogeneity analysis as the second stage of the evaluation decision tree, following quality gate validation and preceding per-class paired analysis. Bhapkar (1966) proposed a slightly more powerful variant of the test for small and sparse tables; the REPDS implementation includes both statistics and defaults to the Bhapkar variant when cell counts drop below a conventional threshold.

Cross-validation methodology for reliable performance estimation is addressed by the scikit-learn user guide (Pedregosa et al., 2011), which describes k-fold and stratified k-fold as the standard approaches for datasets too small to support independent train/validation/test splits. The scikit-learn documentation emphasizes that model selection (hyperparameter tuning) and performance estimation should be separated through nested cross-validation or held-out test sets to prevent optimistic bias. REPDS honors this separation by using the synthetic held-out split for V2 hyperparameter selection during the sweep and the untouched AffectNet real-world set for deployment-tier reporting ŌĆö the AffectNet set is never consulted during training-hyperparameter decisions.

Calibration evaluation is addressed separately because it concerns the alignment between predicted probabilities and empirical event frequencies, not classification accuracy per se. Guo et al. (2017) establish the Expected Calibration Error (ECE) and Brier score as the standard reporting metrics for multi-class calibration and note that modern deep networks are typically *overconfident* and benefit from post-hoc temperature scaling. REPDS Phase 1 incorporates ECE and Brier as Gate A-val requirements and plans temperature scaling as a Phase 2 improvement (┬¦6.1).

## 4. Hypotheses, Experiments, and Data Analysis

### 4.1 Research Hypotheses

Phase 1 is organized around three primary research hypotheses and two secondary research questions, all of which are confined to offline classification, model selection, and deployment nomination.

- **H1 (Gate A-val qualification).** At least one of Variant 1 or Variant 2, when fine-tuned on the curated synthetic dataset and evaluated on the held-out synthetic validation set, will satisfy the Gate A-val thresholds (Macro F1 Ōēź 0.84, Per-class F1 Ōēź 0.75 with floor Ōēź 0.70, Balanced Accuracy Ōēź 0.85, ECE Ōēż 0.12, Brier Ōēż 0.16).

- **H2 (Significant V2 improvement).** Variant 2 will demonstrate statistically significant improvement over Variant 1 on the shared synthetic validation set, as determined by McNemar's test (╬▒ = 0.05), and at least one per-class F1 will improve by a practically significant effect size (Cohen's *h* Ōēź 0.20).

- **H3 (Gate A-deploy qualification).** At least one of V1 or V2 will satisfy the Gate A-deploy threshold (Macro F1 Ōēź 0.75) when evaluated on the untouched AffectNet real-world test set (n = 894), enabling a deployment nomination.

- **RQ1 (Synthetic-to-real generalization gap).** How large is the F1 drop between the synthetic validation set and the AffectNet real-world test set, and is that gap structured (i.e., concentrated on a particular emotion class or confusion pair) or unstructured?

- **RQ2 (Statistical winner vs. deployment winner).** Will the variant with the best aggregate statistics on the real-world test set also be the variant with the safest *per-class error profile* for a downstream gesture-planning system, or will the two criteria select different models?

The primary expectation, based on published benchmarks for EfficientNet-B0 on AffectNet (Savchenko, 2022) and on transfer learning theory (Pan & Yang, 2010; PyTorch Tutorials, 2024), is that H1 and H2 will be confirmed in favor of V2. H3 is expected to hold for at least one variant, but the Gate A-deploy threshold was explicitly lowered from the original 0.84 to 0.75 in ADR 011 precisely because neither variant could reach the synthetic-level threshold on real faces, reflecting an acknowledged generalization gap. RQ2 is the structurally interesting question of this paper: if the aggregate-best and deployment-best models differ, the project must default to deployment safety, and the statistical pipeline must surface the asymmetry explicitly.

### 4.2 Dataset and Preprocessing

The training dataset for Phase 1 consists exclusively of synthetic video clips generated via the Luma AI Dream Machine text-to-video API. Clips were generated using structured prompts specifying: (a) emotion category (`happy`, `sad`, `neutral`); (b) demographic variation (gender presentation, approximate age, skin tone range); (c) environmental context (indoor/outdoor, lighting quality); and (d) emotion intensity (mild, moderate, pronounced). Prompts were iteratively refined to maximize within-class variation and minimize between-class ambiguity in expressed emotion. The final corpus contains 11,911 synthetic videos ŌĆö 3,589 happy, 5,015 sad, and 3,307 neutral ŌĆö each with a nominal duration of five seconds at 24 frames per second.

Face-cropped frame extraction follows the convention established in `trainer/prepare_dataset.py`: ten frames per clip are selected using stratified random sampling within each clip's temporal range, producing a fixed-size representation independent of clip duration variation. Each sampled frame is then passed through an OpenCV DNN SSD face detector; the bounding box with the highest confidence is cropped with a 20 percent padding margin and resized to 224 ├Ś 224 pixels. Frames without a detected face (typically < 3 percent of samples) are discarded. This approach was introduced in run_0107 and produced the largest single-intervention improvement in project history ŌĆö real-world F1 improved from 0.43 to 0.78 and ECE dropped by approximately 37 percent (see `stats/results/runs/analysis_run_0107.md`).

Frames are normalized using ImageNet mean and standard deviation values (╬╝ = [0.485, 0.456, 0.406]; Žā = [0.229, 0.224, 0.225]), consistent with the preprocessing pipeline expected by EfficientNet-B0 pretrained weights. Following face cropping and split, the effective Phase 1 dataset comprises 86,519 training frames and 28,840 validation frames, with class balance inherited from the source video counts weighted by the frames-per-clip constant.

Data augmentation is applied during training to expand effective dataset size and improve generalization. The augmentation pipeline, implemented via Albumentations (Buslaev et al., 2020) and configured by `DataConfig`, applies the following transforms stochastically: horizontal flip (p = 0.5), random rotation (┬▒15┬░, p = 0.4), brightness and contrast jitter (┬▒0.2, p = 0.3), Gaussian blur (kernel 3ŌĆō5, p = 0.2), random crop with resize (scale 0.85ŌĆō1.0, p = 0.3), and Mixup interpolation (╬▒ = 0.2, p = 0.3). Mixup in particular has been shown to improve both robustness and calibration (Zhang et al., 2018, as referenced in the REPDS augmentation literature; M├╝ller et al., 2019).

The dataset split is performed at the *clip* level ŌĆö not the frame level ŌĆö to prevent temporal leakage between splits. All ten frames from a given clip are assigned to the same split. This design prevents the model from memorizing clip-specific background characteristics and evaluating on frames from the same clip, which would artificially inflate validation accuracy. Stratification preserves the 3-class proportion across training and validation subsets.

The real-world evaluation set is a 894-image stratified subset of AffectNet prepared via `trainer/ingest_affectnet.py` and `trainer/create_affectnet_datasets.py`. The class counts are 435 happy, 160 sad, and 299 neutral, reflecting AffectNet's natural distribution for those three categories. Crucially, the AffectNet subset is *never* consumed by any training loop or hyperparameter sweep; it is reserved exclusively for Gate A-deploy reporting and for the statistical comparison presented in ┬¦4.8.

### 4.5 Statistical Analysis Framework

The Phase 1 statistical pipeline, implemented in `stats/scripts/04_phase1_statistical_pipeline.py` and orchestrated by the n8n Evaluation Agent workflow, follows a four-stage decision tree aligned with the `stats/` module. The decision tree is summarized in Figure 2 and Tables 3 and 4 below.

**Stage 1 ŌĆö Two-Tier Gate A Quality Validation.** Prior to any comparative testing, both Variant 1 and Variant 2 checkpoints must independently satisfy the Gate A-val thresholds on the synthetic held-out validation set (script: `01_quality_gate_metrics.py`). A model failing Gate A-val does not proceed to ONNX export; however, per ADR 011, a model may still be evaluated on the real-world AffectNet test set for documentation purposes. Gate A-deploy is then evaluated on AffectNet with the lowered thresholds documented in Table 3.

**Table 3. Two-Tier Gate A Thresholds for Phase 1.**

| Metric | Gate A-val (synthetic) | Gate A-deploy (real-world) |
|--------|------------------------|----------------------------|
| Macro F1 | Ōēź 0.84 | Ōēź 0.75 |
| Per-class F1 | Ōēź 0.75, floor Ōēź 0.70 | Ōēź 0.70, floor Ōēź 0.65 |

**Stage 2 ŌĆö McNemar's Test.** For pairwise comparison of classification accuracy on the shared test set, McNemar's test (McNemar, 1947) is applied using the 2├Ś2 contingency table of paired disagreements. Let *b* = number of instances correctly classified by V1 but not V2, and *c* = number correctly classified by V2 but not V1. The continuity-corrected McNemar statistic is

Žć┬▓ = (|*b* ŌłÆ *c*| ŌłÆ 1)┬▓ / (*b* + *c*)

with one degree of freedom. A significant result (*p* < 0.05) supports rejection of the null hypothesis that V1 and V2 perform equivalently. The REPDS implementation uses `mlxtend.evaluate.mcnemar` (Raschka, 2018). When raw per-item predictions are unavailable on this machine (the predictions `.npz` files reside on the training node), McNemar's test is bounded using the marginal correctness counts ŌĆö a rigorous approach described in detail in ┬¦4.8.2.

**Stage 3 ŌĆö StuartŌĆōMaxwell Test.** The StuartŌĆōMaxwell test (Stuart, 1955; Maxwell, 1970) extends the paired classification comparison to the full 3├Ś3 confusion table, testing whether the marginal distribution of predicted labels differs significantly between V1 and V2. A significant StuartŌĆōMaxwell statistic (*p* < 0.05) indicates that fine-tuning has produced an overall shift in the model's classification behavior, motivating per-class analysis to identify which emotion categories improved or degraded. Bhapkar's (1966) variant is used when cell counts fall below a small-sample threshold. In the present evaluation, the marginal distributions of predicted labels are compared with a chi-square goodness-of-fit proxy (┬¦4.8.3) because per-item paired predictions are unavailable.

**Stage 4 ŌĆö Per-Class Paired Comparison with BenjaminiŌĆōHochberg FDR Correction.** Per-class recall proportions are compared between V1 and V2 using two-proportion *z*-tests (one per emotion category). Because three simultaneous tests are conducted (happy, sad, neutral), the BenjaminiŌĆōHochberg procedure (Benjamini & Hochberg, 1995) is applied to control the false discovery rate at *q* = 0.05. This correction is less conservative than Bonferroni adjustment and provides greater statistical power to detect genuine per-class improvements (Solanas et al., 2024). Where the normality assumption for *t*-tests is questionable ŌĆö as may occur with small counts in rare-class cells ŌĆö the Wilcoxon signed-rank or exact binomial test is substituted as a nonparametric alternative.

### 4.8 Statistical Comparison of V1 and V2

This section implements the Phase 1 statistical decision tree on the shared AffectNet test set. All computations are reproducible from the confusion matrices in ┬¦4.7 via the script excerpted in Appendix A.

#### 4.8.1 Aggregate Accuracy

Direct accuracy comparison: V1 answers 689 of 894 items correctly (77.07 percent); V2 answers 730 of 894 items correctly (81.66 percent). V2 is the more accurate classifier on real-world AffectNet by an absolute 4.59 percentage points. Macro-F1, however, is essentially tied (V1 0.7807 vs V2 0.7798), and balanced accuracy favors V2 (0.8118 vs 0.7994).

#### 4.8.2 McNemar's Test (Bounded Analysis)

Because the raw per-item predictions `.npz` files reside on the training node (`/media/rusty_admin/.../predictions.npz`) and were not mirrored into the Phase 1 repository, the exact McNemar statistic cannot be computed from the test artifacts available in this working tree. However, McNemar's test is tightly bounded by the marginal correctness counts: given that V1 is correct on 689 items and V2 is correct on 730 items, let *n*ŌéüŌéĆ denote the number of items on which V1 is correct but V2 is wrong. Then *n*ŌéĆŌéü = *n*ŌéüŌéĆ + 41 (because *n*ŌéĆŌéü ŌłÆ *n*ŌéüŌéĆ equals the accuracy gap), and the feasible range of *n*ŌéüŌéĆ is [max(0, *w*Ōéü + *w*Ōéé ŌłÆ *n*), min(*w*Ōéü, *w*Ōéé)] = [0, 164], where *w*Ōéü = 205 and *w*Ōéé = 164 are the V1 and V2 error counts respectively.

For each feasible *n*ŌéüŌéĆ value, we compute the continuity-corrected McNemar statistic

Žć┬▓ = (|*n*ŌéüŌéĆ ŌłÆ *n*ŌéĆŌéü| ŌłÆ 1)┬▓ / (*n*ŌéüŌéĆ + *n*ŌéĆŌéü) = (41 ŌłÆ 1)┬▓ / (2 *n*ŌéüŌéĆ + 41) = 1600 / (2 *n*ŌéüŌéĆ + 41)

which is maximized at *n*ŌéüŌéĆ = 0 (Žć┬▓ = 39.02) and minimized at *n*ŌéüŌéĆ = 164 (Žć┬▓ = 4.34). Under the chi-square distribution with 1 degree of freedom, these correspond to *p*-values of 4.2├Ś10Ōü╗┬╣Ōü░ and 3.7├Ś10Ōü╗┬▓, respectively. **Every feasible joint distribution consistent with the observed marginals produces a Žć┬▓ value exceeding the ╬▒ = 0.05 critical value of 3.84.** Therefore, McNemar's test rejects the null hypothesis of equal accuracy on the shared AffectNet test set, and V2 is the significantly more accurate classifier regardless of which paired configuration holds.

This is a stronger statement than the usual point-estimate McNemar because it requires no assumptions about the overlap of V1 and V2 correct-sets. For completeness, the intermediate point estimate ŌĆö corresponding to independent errors ŌĆö has *n*ŌéüŌéĆ Ōēł *w*Ōéü ┬Ę *w*Ōéé / *n* = 205 ┬Ę 164 / 894 Ōēł 37.6, giving Žć┬▓ Ōēł 1600 / 116.2 Ōēł 13.8, *p* Ōēł 2.0├Ś10Ōü╗Ōü┤. This point estimate should be read as "the most plausible single-value McNemar result under error independence"; the bounds above remain the formal conclusion.

On the synthetic validation set, the bounded McNemar computation is even more decisive: *n*ŌéüŌéĆ Ōłł [0, 16], Žć┬▓ Ōłł [228.74, 257.00], *p* < 10Ōü╗ŌüĄŌü░.

#### 4.8.3 Marginal Homogeneity (StuartŌĆōMaxwell Proxy)

A strict StuartŌĆōMaxwell test requires the full paired 3├Ś3 table (*V*Ōéü-pred ├Ś *V*Ōéé-pred on the same items), which is again derived from per-item predictions unavailable in this working tree. We therefore compute a marginal-homogeneity proxy: a chi-square goodness-of-fit test comparing each variant's predicted class marginals to the pooled average.

V1 predicted marginals (from V1 confusion matrix column sums): [278, 161, 455]
V2 predicted marginals (from V2 confusion matrix column sums): [423, 255, 216]
True marginals (AffectNet): [435, 160, 299]
Pooled average: [(278+423)/2, (161+255)/2, (455+216)/2] = [350.5, 208.0, 335.5]

An identical magnitude chi-square results when V2's marginals are tested against the pooled average. This proxy demonstrates that V1 and V2 disagree *systemically* in how they distribute probability mass over the three predicted classes ŌĆö V1 over-predicts neutral (455 vs. 350.5) and under-predicts happy (278 vs. 350.5), while V2 over-predicts sad (255 vs. 208.0) and under-predicts neutral (216 vs. 335.5). The statistical pipeline has established the following facts on the 894-image AffectNet real-world test set:

1. V2 is the significantly more accurate model on aggregate (McNemar *p* < 0.037 under any valid pairing; *p* Ōēł 2├Ś10Ōü╗Ōü┤ under the independence estimate).
2. The two variants produce marginally different predicted-label distributions (marginal homogeneity rejected, *p* = 1.6├Ś10Ōü╗┬╣ŌüĄ).
3. The per-class analysis shows that V2 wins strongly on happy (*h* = +0.77) and loses strongly on neutral (*h* = ŌłÆ0.86), with the sad class non-significant after BH correction.
4. Both variants clear the Gate A-deploy macro-F1 threshold of 0.75.
5. V1 meets all three per-class F1 thresholds at the Ōēź 0.70 floor (0.777, 0.822, 0.743); V2 falls below 0.70 on sad (0.694) but meets the relaxed 0.65 floor.

Under a criterion of aggregate accuracy alone, V2 wins. Under a criterion of per-class balance relative to the deploy-tier thresholds, V1 wins. The deployment decision therefore rests on which criterion is prioritized by the downstream system.

Phase 2 of the project consumes the Phase 1 model via a confidence-aware gesture planner (see `apps/reachy/gestures/gesture_modulator.py` in the Phase 2 codebase). The planner maps each emotion classification to one of five gesture tiers. A false positive on the *sad* class ŌĆö calling a neutral person sad ŌĆö causes the robot to emit empathy/comfort/hug gestures that are inappropriate for a non-distressed user. V2 commits this error at a rate of 105/299 (35.1 percent), compared to 18/299 (6.0 percent) for V1. This asymmetric cost structure, documented in the run_0107 analysis report, tilts the deployment decision toward V1.

Additional deployment-relevance considerations:

- **V1's residual errors lie on the happyŌåöneutral boundary** (147 of V1's 205 errors are happy misclassified as neutral). Misreading a happy person as neutral causes the robot to default to a listening/nodding gesture rather than a celebratory one, which is subjectively a much milder error than empathy-gesturing at a non-distressed user.
- **V2's residual errors lie on the neutralŌåösad boundary.** Misreading neutral as sad causes the robot to produce empathy-coded gestures (comfort, hug, listen) ŌĆö interventions the user did not invite.
- **V1 almost never confuses happy and sad** (only 11 happyŌåÆsad errors and 0 sadŌåÆhappy errors). V2 confuses across the full triangle (23 happyŌåÆneutral, 13 sadŌåÆneutral, 105 neutralŌåÆsad, 14 neutralŌåÆhappy).

The nominated deployment model for Phase 1 is therefore **Variant 1, run_0107**. This is the model that will be exported to ONNX and consumed by the Phase 3 TensorRT conversion pipeline. The nomination is recorded in `memory-bank/decisions/011-two-tier-gate-a-v1-deployment.md` and `stats/results/sweep/best_v2_sweep_summary.json` (which explicitly notes that V2's sweep winner has superior synthetic statistics but loses on the real-world per-class balance test).

## 5. Threats to the Validity of the Study

Phase 1 of REPDS is a statistically disciplined but deliberately scoped offline experiment. Its design choices create a specific set of validity threats that must be documented explicitly, both for pedagogical completeness and because the deployment nomination in ┬¦4.9 depends on a reader understanding the limits of the evidence. Following the conventional Cook-and-Campbell taxonomy, the threats are grouped into four categories: internal validity (are observed differences genuinely caused by the Variant 1 vs. Variant 2 contrast?), external validity (do findings generalize beyond the sampled conditions?), construct validity (do the metrics measure what they purport to measure?), and statistical conclusion validity (are inferential procedures applied correctly and interpreted conservatively?).

### 5.1 Internal Validity

The principal internal-validity concern in Phase 1 is the extent to which differences measured between V1 and V2 can be attributed strictly to the *fine-tuning mechanism* (selective unfreezing of `blocks.5`, `blocks.6`, and `conv_head` with a differential learning rate) rather than to secondary factors that co-vary with the V1 ŌåÆ V2 transition. Three specific co-variations deserve scrutiny.

First, V2 is initialized from the best V1 checkpoint, not trained from scratch. This design is intentional ŌĆö it mirrors the standard feature-extraction-then-fine-tuning recipe (PyTorch Tutorials, 2024) and ensures that the V1 head is carried forward ŌĆö but it means that V2 inherits any V1 training stochasticity. The fixed random seed (42) and deterministic-mode flag partially mitigate this, but do not eliminate the confound: V2's starting point is "wherever V1 ended up at epoch 23." A strictly counterfactual design would train V2 from HSEmotion initialization directly with the fine-tuning schedule, never loading V1's head. That experiment is deferred to a sensitivity analysis and is not part of the current run_0107 results.

Second, the hyperparameter sweep for V2 is absent for V1. Variant 1's hyperparameters were held at the project defaults documented in `trainer/fer_finetune/specs/efficientnet_b0_emotion_3cls.yaml` (see `trainer/train_variant1.py:59` for the resolved `TrainingConfig`), whereas Variant 2 was optimized across 101 trials. If either variant "should" benefit from sweep-level exploration, it is V1 ŌĆö the feature-extraction head has its own learning rate, label-smoothing, and Mixup ╬▒ that could plausibly be re-tuned. The decision not to sweep V1 reflects a design commitment: V1 is the frozen-backbone baseline, deliberately held fixed to provide a reproducible reference point for V2. A future ablation should nonetheless explore whether a swept V1 can approach swept V2 on either synthetic or real-world metrics.

Third, the DatasetPreparer pipeline (`trainer/prepare_dataset.py`) was modified *between* early V1 runs and run_0107 to enforce face cropping via OpenCV DNN SSD before downstream resizing. This preprocessing intervention produced the largest single-run improvement in project history (synthetic F1 Ōēł +0.16 on V1, real-world F1 Ōēł +0.35 on both variants). The run_0107 window compares V1 and V2 *both trained under the face-cropped pipeline*, so the internal comparison is fair. However, the researcher should remain alert to the possibility that any future change to the preprocessing pipeline could invalidate the V1ŌåöV2 deltas reported in ┬¦4.6ŌĆō┬¦4.8.

A fourth, subtler internal-validity concern is the single-researcher labeling of the curated synthetic corpus. The clips promoted into `videos/train/<label>/` were classified by the same researcher who designed the model architecture and read the sweep leaderboard. This creates a channel for unconscious selection bias: clips that "look like what the model wants to see" may be preferentially promoted, inflating training-distributionŌĆōtest-distribution alignment on the synthetic side. The real-world AffectNet evaluation is unaffected (it is an externally labeled fixed set), so the practical impact is bounded; but the synthetic-tier Gate A-val metrics (F1 Ōēł 0.99) should be interpreted with this in mind. Two mitigations are planned: (a) a second-labeler audit on a random 10 percent sample of the curated corpus, and (b) a dataset-hash-stamped manifest (already implemented in `DatasetPreparer`) that enables rollback if an audit reveals systematic mislabeling.

### 5.2 External Validity

External validity is the most consequential category of threat to Phase 1, because the entire purpose of the nomination decision is to project offline results onto an in-the-wild deployment context. Four external-validity concerns are foregrounded.

First, the *synthetic-to-real generalization gap* documented in ┬¦4.7 is substantial: macro F1 drops from approximately 0.99 on synthetic validation to approximately 0.78 on the 894-image AffectNet real-world test. A gap of ~21 F1 points is larger than one would expect from a well-matched domain transfer and signals that the Luma AI generative-video distribution does not fully cover the real-face expression manifold. The face-cropping intervention closed a large fraction of the prior gap, but a residual gap remains. Phase 1 has no mechanism to close this further ŌĆö addressing it requires either (a) diversifying the synthetic corpus along dimensions the model is still sensitive to, (b) mixing a small amount of real data into training, or (c) applying a post-hoc calibration layer on real-world data only. All three interventions are out of Phase 1 scope.

Second, the AffectNet test subset (n = 894) is a *stratified* subset chosen via `trainer/create_affectnet_datasets.py` and `trainer/ingest_affectnet.py`. Stratification preserves the 3-class structure but does not guarantee demographic or contextual coverage matching that of a deployed Reachy Mini. AffectNet is biased toward frontal studio images at moderate resolution; Reachy Mini will encounter oblique angles, low light, partial occlusion, and non-frontal poses. The reported F1 scores should therefore be read as *upper bounds* on real-world deployment performance under comparable conditions, and the Phase 3 shadow-mode rollout will be the first opportunity to observe an unbiased in-the-wild distribution.

Third, demographic fidelity of the Luma AI generative model is not independently characterized. Large text-to-video models trained on internet-sourced data are known to exhibit biases in gender presentation, skin tone distribution, age, and expression intensity. The generation prompts were explicitly written to vary these attributes, but prompt conditioning is a weak instrument for forcing coverage ŌĆö the model's prior over "happy face" is not guaranteed to respect the researcher's demographic target. If the synthetic corpus systematically underrepresents, for example, darker skin tones or older faces, the deployed model may exhibit differential accuracy on those populations in the field. This is an active area of concern for the Phase 2 ingest roadmap, where a demographic-balance audit of the curated corpus is planned.

Fourth, the 3-class scope (happy / sad / neutral) is a deliberate reduction of Ekman's (1992) 6-class taxonomy. Phase 1 is therefore silent on how the selected model would perform on anger, fear, disgust, surprise, or contempt ŌĆö classes that matter for a companion robot in distress-monitoring contexts. The nominated V1 checkpoint cannot simply be extended to 8-class without retraining; the reserved-class data does not exist yet. External generalization to the broader Ekman taxonomy is Phase 2+ work and is *not* supported by the Phase 1 statistical evidence.

### 5.3 Construct Validity

Construct-validity threats concern the mapping between what the metrics measure and what the deployed system needs to accomplish. Two concerns dominate.

First, the Gate A-val macro F1 threshold (Ōēź 0.84) and Gate A-deploy threshold (Ōēź 0.75) are both *aggregate* metrics. They do not encode the asymmetric cost structure of downstream gesture planning. The deployment-safety analysis in ┬¦4.9 ŌĆö which tilts the nomination toward V1 despite V2's better aggregate statistics ŌĆö arises *because* the F1 threshold under-specifies the deployment criterion. A more construct-valid formulation would define a cost matrix over the 3├Ś3 confusion table (e.g., neutralŌåÆsad is 3├Ś more costly than happyŌåÆneutral) and optimize an expected-loss metric. This is not implemented in Phase 1 because the cost weights require Phase 2 gesture-planner and user-study data to calibrate. The current pipeline is therefore construct-undercomplete: it reports F1 and then manually cross-checks the confusion matrix for structural safety, rather than encoding safety in the selection metric itself.

Second, calibration metrics (ECE, Brier, MCE) measure the agreement between predicted softmax confidence and empirical accuracy, but do not guarantee that the *ordering* of confidences on a single sample is semantically meaningful for gesture modulation. A model with low ECE can still produce the "wrong" ranking of second-most-likely class, which matters when the gesture planner uses a secondary-emotion cue for nuanced modulation. Phase 1 does not evaluate top-2 agreement or secondary-class rank stability, both of which are candidate Phase 2 metrics.

### 5.4 Statistical Conclusion Validity

The statistical pipeline described in ┬¦4.5 is rigorous in its core structure, but three conclusion-validity caveats must be flagged.

First, McNemar's test on the 894-image AffectNet set was computed using *marginal bounds* rather than the standard paired-contingency formula, because per-item paired predictions (`predictions.npz`) reside on the training node and were unavailable on the analysis machine at the time of run_0107 reporting. The bounds are mathematically rigorous ŌĆö the report in ┬¦4.8.2 explicitly gives Žć┬▓ Ōłł [4.34, 39.02] and *p* Ōłł [4.2├Ś10Ōü╗┬╣Ōü░, 3.7├Ś10Ōü╗┬▓] ŌĆö and the null of accuracy equivalence is rejected under every feasible pairing. The conclusion ("V2 is significantly more accurate on aggregate") is therefore valid, but the *point estimate* of Žć┬▓ is not available and the true *p*-value could lie anywhere in the reported interval. For final publication, the analysis will be rerun with the per-item predictions reconstituted from the `stats/results/` artifacts, and the exact McNemar Žć┬▓ recorded in the final manuscript.

Second, the StuartŌĆōMaxwell test proxy described in ┬¦4.8.3 is a marginal-homogeneity chi-square rather than the full StuartŌĆōMaxwell statistic, again due to per-item predictions being unavailable. The proxy is *more conservative* than StuartŌĆōMaxwell (it ignores the paired structure and therefore has lower power), so a rejection at *p* = 1.6├Ś10Ōü╗┬╣ŌüĄ is a *floor* on the significance ŌĆö the true StuartŌĆōMaxwell statistic would produce a smaller *p*-value, not a larger one. The reported conclusion (rejection of marginal homogeneity) is therefore safe, but the machinery will be upgraded to full StuartŌĆōMaxwell + Bhapkar in the final manuscript.

Third, the per-class two-proportion *z*-tests with BH correction in ┬¦4.8.4 assume independence of the two models' per-sample errors *within* a class. Because V2 is initialized from V1 ŌĆö i.e., the two models share an initialization and a portion of their feature extractor ŌĆö the independence assumption is not literally satisfied. The correct paired test for this setting is a per-class McNemar test on the 894-sample contingency structure. When per-item predictions are restored, this upgrade is straightforward and will be added to the final pipeline. For the current report, the *z*-test results should be read as a *conservative approximation*: the paired McNemar would typically produce *smaller* *p*-values (higher power) at the same effect size, so the pattern of significance reported in Table 8 is unlikely to change qualitatively.

Fourth and finally, the sample size for sad-class inference on AffectNet (n = 160) is modest. The sad-class recall shift (╬ö = +0.075, Cohen's *h* = +0.22) does not survive BH correction (*p*_BH = 0.0514), and the 95 percent confidence interval on the difference includes zero. A larger real-world test set ŌĆö in particular, pulling additional sad samples from AffectNet's full corpus or from RAF-DB (Li et al., 2017) ŌĆö would be necessary to resolve the sad-class comparison definitively.

## 6. Future Work

Future work is organized along two axes: (i) *within-Phase-1 hardening*, which extends the current experimental design without changing its scope, and (ii) *cross-phase extensions*, which describe the planned handoff to Phase 2 (emotional intelligence layer) and Phase 3 (edge deployment) as they consume the Phase 1 nominated artifact. This paper commits only to Phase 1 deliverables; the Phase 2 and Phase 3 discussions below are provided solely to place the nomination in the broader project context.

### 6.1 Within-Phase-1 Hardening

The highest-priority within-Phase-1 work items are those that directly address validity threats identified in ┬¦5, without expanding the experimental scope beyond offline 3-class classification.

**Full-resolution statistical pipeline.** The McNemar, StuartŌĆōMaxwell, and per-class analyses in ┬¦4.8 should be rerun with per-item prediction arrays (`predictions.npz`) restored on the analysis node. The four-stage pipeline in `stats/scripts/04_phase1_statistical_pipeline.py` is already designed to consume these artifacts; the only gap is synchronizing the training-node output directory with the analysis-node input directory. Once this is resolved, the marginal-bound formulation of McNemar will be replaced with the exact statistic, the StuartŌĆōMaxwell proxy will be replaced with the full StuartŌĆōMaxwell and Bhapkar statistics, and the per-class *z*-tests will be replaced with per-class paired McNemar. None of these upgrades is expected to qualitatively reverse the pattern of significance reported in ┬¦4.8 ŌĆö the conservative bounds already reject the null ŌĆö but the exact values should be in the final manuscript for completeness and reproducibility (Solanas et al., 2024).

**V1 hyperparameter ablation sweep.** A narrow sweep over V1's head-only hyperparameters (head learning rate Ōłł {1├Ś10Ōü╗Ōü┤, 3├Ś10Ōü╗Ōü┤, 1├Ś10Ōü╗┬│}, dropout Ōłł {0.2, 0.3, 0.4}, label smoothing Ōłł {0.05, 0.10, 0.15}) will establish whether a *tuned* V1 can approach or exceed the swept V2 on the AffectNet per-class balance criterion. If a tuned V1 improves its neutral-class recall without losing happy-class recall, the deployment case for V1 is strengthened; if V2's per-class structure is invariant to V1 sweep position, the case for V2's aggregate dominance is strengthened. Either outcome sharpens the nomination decision. This sweep reuses the existing `trainer/sweep_variant2.py` machinery with the V1 training entry point substituted, and is estimated at Ōēł 30 GPU-hours.

**Second-labeler audit on curated corpus.** A random 10 percent sample of the promoted `videos/train/<label>/` clips will be re-labeled by a second, independent annotator blind to the original label. Agreement will be reported as Cohen's ╬║ and as per-class confusion counts. A ╬║ < 0.80 or any systematic per-class bias will trigger a relabeling campaign against the full corpus. This task is coordinated by the n8n Labeling Agent (agent #2 in `AGENTS.md`) and requires operator support; it is tractable within the Phase 1 window.

**Demographic audit of the synthetic corpus.** A scripted pass through the curated clips will apply an off-the-shelf demographic attribute classifier (age bucket, gender presentation, Fitzpatrick skin tone bucket) and emit a distribution report. The goal is not to rebalance ŌĆö the 3-class emotion balance is already maintained ŌĆö but to characterize where the Luma AI generation distribution is thin. Thin cells then become the target of a second-round prompt refinement using the Luma API. This closes the "unknown demographic fidelity" external-validity threat with direct empirical evidence rather than speculation.

**Expanded real-world test set.** Two augmentations to the AffectNet-only evaluation are planned. First, increase the AffectNet sad stratum from 160 to Ōēł 300 by sampling additional sad examples via `trainer/create_affectnet_datasets.py` ŌĆö this raises the sad-class per-class *z*-test power enough to resolve the currently marginal *p*_BH = 0.0514 comparison. Second, add RAF-DB (Li et al., 2017) as a second real-world test set, processed through the same face-cropping pipeline. RAF-DB's "compound emotion" annotations provide an opportunity to stress-test the 3-class reduction on naturally ambiguous samples and to report an independent external-dataset F1 alongside the AffectNet result.

**Temperature calibration layer.** Per Guo et al. (2017), post-hoc temperature scaling on a held-out calibration subset is a cheap, model-invariant intervention that typically reduces ECE by 30ŌĆō60 percent without affecting rank-accuracy. A temperature-scaled V1 run_0107 is expected to clear Gate A-val on ECE (currently failing at 0.1242) without any retraining. This intervention is straightforward ŌĆö a single scalar fit via cross-entropy on the validation softmax logits ŌĆö and closes the last Gate A-val failure without re-running training.

### 6.2 Handoff to Phase 2 (Emotional Intelligence Layer)

Phase 2 is out of scope for the present paper, but the nominated V1 run_0107 checkpoint is the *input artifact* that Phase 2 consumes, and several integration requirements feed back into how Phase 1 must archive its results.

Phase 2 implements the *Degree / PPE / EQ* layer described in `memory-bank/requirements.md` ┬¦9.5 and in `CLAUDE.md` Phase 2. The layer uses (a) the per-sample softmax probability vector produced by the Phase 1 classifier as a continuous "degree of emotion" signal, (b) a 5-tier confidence bucketing (`apps/reachy/gestures/gesture_modulator.py`) that drives amplitude, speed, and head-tilt modulation of the Reachy Mini gesture library, and (c) an emotion-conditioned LLM prompt template (`apps/llm/prompts/emotion_prompts.py`) that tailors dialogue tone to the classified emotion and its confidence. For Phase 1 to feed Phase 2 correctly, the nominated checkpoint must export:

- A softmax output layer with *non-temperature-scaled* logits available for downstream calibration fitting, in addition to the scaled probabilities used by the gesture modulator.
- The Phase 1 run metadata JSON (`stats/results/runs/train/var1_run_0107.json`) including the dataset hash, sweep position, and per-class calibration metrics, so that Phase 2 can detect retraining drift.
- A per-sample AffectNet prediction file recording top-3 class indices and probabilities, so that Phase 2 can compute top-2 agreement and secondary-rank stability ŌĆö the secondary-class metrics identified as Phase 2 construct-validity requirements in ┬¦5.3.

These are archival extensions, not scientific extensions, and are already supported by the `save_training_artifacts` function in `trainer/save_run_artifacts.py:1` invoked at the end of `trainer/train_variant1.py:197`. The Phase 2 feedback loop is therefore ready to consume the Phase 1 nomination the moment the final manuscript is signed off.

### 6.3 Handoff to Phase 3 (Edge Deployment)

Phase 3 converts the nominated PyTorch checkpoint to ONNX and then to a TensorRT FP16 engine for the Jetson Xavier NX, integrates the engine into the DeepStream pipeline, and runs Gates B (latency + memory) and C (shadow-to-canary rollout). Three Phase 1 decisions must be validated against Phase 3 constraints before the nomination is considered final.

**ONNX export compatibility.** EfficientNet-B0's inverted-residual blocks are known-good for TensorRT 8.x FP16 export, so the structural risk is low. A smoke-test export of the V1 run_0107 checkpoint should be performed early in Phase 3 to confirm that the `conv_head` and global-average-pool fusion lower correctly; any operator incompatibility discovered at this stage would force a Phase 1 re-export, which is preferable to discovering it after gesture-planner integration.

**FP16 accuracy retention.** The FP32 PyTorch checkpoint achieves F1 = 0.7807 on AffectNet; the FP16 TensorRT engine is expected to drop by < 0.5 percent (Li et al., 2020; NVIDIA, 2023), leaving F1 Ōēł 0.776. This is still above the Gate A-deploy threshold of 0.75, but the margin is thin. Phase 3 should compute the exact FP16 AffectNet F1 and compare it against the FP32 reference *before* declaring the engine ready for shadow deployment. A one-time calibration pass with the TensorRT entropy calibrator on a held-out subset of the synthetic validation frames is recommended to minimize quantization drift.

**Preprocessing parity.** The Phase 1 face-cropping pipeline runs in Python (`trainer/prepare_dataset.py`) using OpenCV DNN SSD. The Jetson pipeline must reproduce this preprocessing *exactly* ŌĆö any drift in crop margin, interpolation mode, or normalization constants will produce a distribution shift between training and deployment that is not caused by the model. The Phase 3 DeepStream config (`jetson/deepstream/emotion_pipeline.txt`) should therefore explicitly mirror the crop-margin, target-size, and ImageNet mean/std constants used by DatasetPreparer. A dedicated parity test ŌĆö running the same N images through both pipelines and verifying pixel-level equivalence ŌĆö is a Phase 3 entry criterion.

### 6.4 Long-Term Extensions

Several extensions lie beyond even Phase 3 but are anticipated as logical next steps.

**Expanded emotion taxonomy.** The 3-class scope is a stepping stone to the full 6-class Ekman taxonomy (Ekman, 1992) and eventually an 8-class extended set including contempt. Generating sad, fear, disgust, and contempt clips from Luma AI will require prompt engineering that pushes against the generative model's prior over "pleasant" content; an adversarial prompt-refinement loop may be necessary to produce training-quality synthetic video in these rare classes.

**Temporal aggregation.** The current frame-level classifier discards temporal structure within the 5-second clip. A small temporal attention head or even a simple per-clip averaging with uncertainty-weighted fusion could exploit the existing 10-frames-per-clip sampling without architectural change. Prior work (Wang et al., 2016; Tran et al., 2015) shows consistent 2ŌĆō4 F1 point improvements from temporal aggregation on emotion trajectories; this is a high-value, low-risk extension for Phase 2+.

**Federated calibration.** The residual synthetic-to-real gap could be addressed by a federated learning layer in which a small number of opt-in Reachy Mini deployments contribute gradient updates from locally-processed face embeddings (not raw video) to a federated aggregator. This preserves the privacy-first architecture while closing the generalization gap. The technical cost is substantial ŌĆö federated gradient compression, differential-privacy noise injection, aggregation cadence ŌĆö but the design is compatible with the existing 3-node LAN architecture and would not require cloud dependencies.

**Dimensional emotion representation.** The discrete 3-class (eventually 6/8-class) taxonomy can coexist with a *continuous* valenceŌĆōarousal representation in the sense of Russell's (1980) circumplex model. The HSEmotion pretraining actually includes VA regression heads that are discarded in the current V1 / V2 architecture. A multi-task extension that keeps both heads active would produce richer emotional signals for the Phase 2 gesture planner and LLM prompt tailoring, without requiring additional data. This is attractive as a "free" extension to explore in Phase 2 once Phase 1 is closed out.

To prevent scope creep, the following are explicitly *not* Phase 1 deliverables and are deferred to later phases or abandoned entirely:

## 7. Reflections

The Phase 1 experience has yielded several methodological and practical insights that extend beyond the specific technical artifacts of REPDS. This section reports them in first person where appropriate, consistent with the reflective-writing convention in DS 796 capstone reports.

### 7.1 The Disproportionate Cost of Integration

The single most consequential lesson of Phase 1 is that the individual components of the pipeline ŌĆö the `trainer/` training scripts, the `stats/` statistical pipeline, the `apps/web/` Streamlit curation UI, the FastAPI gateway, the n8n orchestration workflows, the MLflow tracker ŌĆö *each function correctly in isolation*, and collectively took a small fraction of the total project time to build. Making them communicate reliably and reproducibly as a *system* required substantially more engineering effort than building any single component. The face-cropping intervention that produced the single largest accuracy jump in the entire project was not a model change ŌĆö it was a preprocessing pipeline change that had to propagate simultaneously through `DatasetPreparer`, the per-run training manifest format, the validation split construction, and the evaluation-side preprocessing. Finding and fixing the mismatch between train-time and evaluation-time preprocessing took longer than writing either code path in isolation.

This matches the broad observation in applied ML engineering that end-to-end integration is the dominant cost center, and it argues for a specific project-management discipline: *allocate at least 40 percent of project time to integration and regression testing from the beginning*, not as a cleanup phase at the end. The na├»ve 15ŌĆō20 percent integration budget that a student typically estimates at project outset is consistently short by a factor of two. On REPDS, I reserved time for integration only after a first pass at the components; the schedule slipped until an explicit integration-first regime was adopted in the run_0106 ŌåÆ run_0107 transition.

### 7.2 Statistical Rigor as a Check on Premature Commitment

The second major lesson is that a disciplined statistical pipeline catches errors of *overinterpretation* that aggregate accuracy monitoring never exposes. Early in Phase 1, before the two-tier Gate A policy was codified in ADR 011, there was a temptation to nominate Variant 2 simply because it had better top-line macro F1 on both the synthetic validation set and the AffectNet real-world test set. The numbers support that conclusion at the aggregate level, and a less rigorous workflow would have ended there.

It was only when the per-class analysis in ┬¦4.8.4 was produced ŌĆö specifically, the table showing that V2 misclassifies 105 of 299 neutral samples as *sad* ŌĆö that the downstream consequence for the Phase 2 gesture planner became visible. A model that commits empathy/comfort gestures to 35 percent of neutral-faced users is a model that will generate a specific, legible, user-reported failure mode in deployment, regardless of its macro F1. The McNemar, StuartŌĆōMaxwell, and per-class paired tests were not just statistical formalities; they were the instruments that surfaced a deployment-safety asymmetry that aggregate-metric monitoring had hidden.

This is a general lesson about the role of statistics in applied ML: significance tests are not about "proving" a model is good, they are about *preventing false confidence*. The temptation to read aggregate accuracy and stop there is strong, and the only reliable defense is a pre-committed evaluation pipeline that the researcher is required to run before declaring a result. On REPDS, the pre-commitment was encoded in `stats/scripts/04_phase1_statistical_pipeline.py` and in the Evaluation Agent workflow ŌĆö not in the researcher's discipline alone.

### 7.3 Synthetic Data as a Research Paradigm: Genuine Trade-offs

The choice to train exclusively on Luma AI synthetic video was motivated primarily by privacy (no IRB, no real-subject retention, no biometric egress) and secondarily by cost (no labeling budget, infinite scalability). Both motivations held up: the project was executed end-to-end without ever touching a real human subject, and the curation loop scaled from a few dozen to ~12,000 clips without budget pressure.

The trade-offs, however, were larger than I anticipated. The synthetic-to-real F1 gap of ~21 points is not a debugging artifact ŌĆö it reflects a genuine distributional mismatch between what Luma AI considers "happy face" and what AffectNet annotators consider "happy face." Nikolenko's (2021) synthetic-data-for-deep-learning framework warns that synthetic diversity must be actively managed, and Phase 1 bore this out: the first-pass prompt library produced visually convincing clips that still under-covered the space of expression intensities (synthetic clips cluster at moderate intensity; real faces span from barely-perceptible to exaggerated), demographic variations, and non-frontal poses. The face-cropping intervention closed much of the gap, but a residual gap remained ŌĆö and will remain until Phase 2 introduces either corpus diversification or calibration against real data.

If I were starting a second synthetic-data project tomorrow, the single change I would make at project outset is to *front-load a diversity audit of the generative pipeline before generating the full corpus*. Generating 12,000 clips takes real API budget; discovering at F1-evaluation time that the generative model has a blind spot forces either (a) regenerating a fraction of the corpus under a revised prompt library, or (b) accepting a known distributional hole. Both are expensive. A small-scale diversity audit (200 clips, stratified by target attribute) in Week 1 would have identified the blind spot in hours rather than weeks.

### 7.4 Most Rewarding Aspects

Two aspects of Phase 1 stood out as genuinely rewarding, independent of their technical contribution to the final result.

The first was the *design of the statistical decision tree itself*. The convergence of machine-learning theory, classical inferential statistics, and domain-specific deployment considerations into a single coherent four-stage pipeline (Gate A ŌåÆ McNemar ŌåÆ StuartŌĆōMaxwell ŌåÆ per-class paired) is the kind of work that feels like intellectual synthesis rather than implementation. The moment when the pipeline ran end-to-end on run_0107 predictions and produced a numerical answer consistent with the manual confusion-matrix inspection ŌĆö and, more importantly, produced it *automatically* for the next run as well ŌĆö was the clearest "this is real science now" moment of the project.

The second was observing the large Cohen's *h* effect size on the happy class (+0.77) simultaneously with the large negative effect size on the neutral class (ŌłÆ0.86). Those numbers are not subtle ŌĆö they are two "very large" effects pointing in opposite directions on two of three classes. A project that had stopped at aggregate F1 would have missed this structure entirely. Seeing the two effects emerge from a mechanical application of Cohen's (1988) *h* formula felt like the statistics *earning their keep*, rather than being a ritual applied after the decision was already made.

### 7.5 Most Challenging Aspects

The two hardest things about Phase 1 were methodological, not technical.

First, *resisting the urge to tune after looking at results*. The V2 sweep produced a composite-best configuration (`var2_sweep_s2_t004`) that was optimized for synthetic Gate A-val. After seeing the sweep winner's real-world per-class structure, it was tempting to rerun the sweep with a real-world per-class component in the composite score ŌĆö a change that would arguably produce a better model but would also constitute circular evaluation, because it would be tuning on the very test set used to validate the Phase 1 nomination. The discipline to *not* make that change, and to instead document the asymmetry in the paper, is harder than the discipline to make it. The project's statistical validity depended on keeping the AffectNet test set untouched by the sweep loop, and that discipline required ignoring a tempting short-term improvement.

Second, *accepting that the statistically best model is not always the deployed model*. The McNemar result on the AffectNet set decisively favors V2 in aggregate, and the student's instinct is to ship the statistically best model. The deployment-safety analysis in ┬¦4.9, however, argues for V1 because V1's residual errors lie on a psychologically safer confusion boundary. Writing that argument down ŌĆö and writing it in a way that a reviewer trained to equate "statistical winner" with "correct answer" would still accept ŌĆö took multiple drafts and forced a clarification of what "correct" even means in a deployment-constrained study. The final resolution is the explicit separation of *Gate A-val / Gate A-deploy statistical tests* from a *deployment-safety cross-check on the confusion matrix*. That separation is, I think, the most transferable methodological contribution of Phase 1.

### 7.6 Recommendations for Future Students

For future DS 796 students undertaking comprehensive data science capstone projects, five recommendations are offered, each grounded in a specific Phase 1 experience:

1. **Pre-commit your statistical pipeline in code before running any experiment.** Write `stats/scripts/04_phase1_statistical_pipeline.py` or its equivalent *first*, wire it into the evaluation agent, and treat it as an immutable binding commitment. Once results are in hand, the temptation to "adjust" the evaluation framework is overwhelming, and the only defense is having already committed to the framework in version-controlled code.

2. **Budget integration testing at 40 percent, not 15 percent.** Every hour saved by skipping an end-to-end integration test will be spent debugging a silent mismatch between components later. Build the end-to-end pipeline smoke test from day one, even if the models inside it are stubs.

3. **Use MLflow (or equivalent) from the first training run.** The cost of rebuilding experiment history from informal notes is orders of magnitude greater than the cost of configuring an experiment tracker at project inception. MLflow's dataset-hash-plus-run-id linkage is what made run-to-run comparisons and the V1ŌåöV2 ablation feasible on REPDS.

4. **Treat data quality and preprocessing as first-class research contributions.** The face-cropping preprocessing intervention was worth more than any model change on REPDS. Data pipeline work is not "glue" ŌĆö it is a primary surface for performance improvement and for reproducibility failures.

5. **Report effect sizes, not just *p*-values.** A *p* = 0.005 with Cohen's *h* Ōēł 0.1 is a trivial effect that happened to reach significance because *n* is large. A *p* = 0.05 with Cohen's *h* Ōēł 2.0 is a transformative effect that narrowly missed a threshold. The significance test alone tells you neither; the effect size tells you both.

### 7.7 Closing Note

Phase 1 of REPDS ends with a specific, defensible, appropriately qualified deliverable: *Variant 1, run_0107, is nominated for export to ONNX and downstream TensorRT conversion*, subject to the within-Phase-1 hardening items listed in ┬¦6.1 and the Phase 3 entry criteria in ┬¦6.3. The nomination is made with the explicit acknowledgment that V2 is the statistically better model in aggregate but that its residual error structure is unsafe for the downstream gesture planner. That asymmetry ŌĆö statistical winner ŌēĀ deployment winner ŌĆö is the single most important finding of Phase 1 and the most valuable lesson it offers to future projects operating under similar deployment-safety constraints.

## Addendum: Citation Justification and Applicability

This addendum provides a per-reference justification linking each cited work to the specific Phase 1 design or analysis decision it informed. The table is organized by the logical role the reference plays in the paper (theoretical foundation, architectural choice, methodological recipe, statistical test, or external benchmark) rather than by alphabetic order, to make the reasoning traceable.

### A.1 Theoretical and Conceptual Foundations

**Picard (1997)** ŌĆö *Affective Computing*. The foundational monograph establishing affective computing as a research field. Cited in ┬¦1.1 and ┬¦3.1 to motivate the entire REPDS project premise: machines that perceive and respond to human emotional states offer qualitatively richer interaction than task-completion-only systems. Picard's framing of emotional attunement as a *design objective* rather than an epiphenomenon directly shapes the REPDS decision to treat emotion recognition as a primary deployment capability rather than a feature added post hoc.

**Ekman (1992)** ŌĆö *An argument for basic emotions*. Establishes the 6-class discrete emotion taxonomy and FACS grounding. Cited in ┬¦3.1 and ┬¦5.2 to justify REPDS Phase 1's reduction to a 3-class subset (happy, sad, neutral) as a principled truncation of a well-established taxonomy rather than an arbitrary simplification. Ekman's claim of cross-cultural universality of basic emotions is also the basis on which face-cropped synthetic training data can plausibly generalize to real faces at all.

**Russell (1980)** ŌĆö *Circumplex model of affect*. The canonical dimensional (valence ├Ś arousal) alternative to Ekman's discrete taxonomy. Cited in ┬¦3.1 and ┬¦6.4 as the theoretical grounding for the Phase 2 degree-of-emotion extension: even though Phase 1 uses discrete classification, the downstream Phase 2 gesture modulator and LLM prompt tailoring depend on a continuous intensity axis that maps cleanly onto Russell's arousal dimension. **Breazeal (2003)** ŌĆö *Toward sociable robots*. The Kismet-era foundation for affective robotics. Cited in ┬¦3.1 to situate REPDS within the emotion-aware social-robotics tradition and to establish precedent for emotion-modulated gesture as a design pattern predating REPDS by two decades.

**Abdi et al. (2018)**; **Leite et al. (2013)**; **Scassellati et al. (2012)**. These three domain-specific surveys (eldercare, long-term interaction, autism research) are cited in ┬¦1.3 and ┬¦3.1 to document beneficiary communities for privacy-preserving emotion recognition. Each is used not for a technical claim but to substantiate the stakeholder analysis: emotion-aware robots have demonstrated usefulness in clinical and educational contexts that depend on local, private deployment ŌĆö the exact constraint REPDS's architecture addresses.

**Pollen Robotics (2025)**. Platform documentation for the Reachy Mini robot. Cited in ┬¦1.1 as the target deployment platform. **McNemar (1947)** ŌĆö *Note on the sampling error of the difference between correlated proportions*. Cited in ┬¦3.6 and ┬¦4.5 as the more powerful variant of StuartŌĆōMaxwell used when cell counts are small or the paired contingency is sparse. **Pedregosa et al. (2011)** ŌĆö *Scikit-learn*. Guo et al.'s observation that modern deep networks are systematically overconfident ŌĆö and that post-hoc temperature scaling is a cheap and effective fix ŌĆö is the reason ECE is a gating metric in REPDS rather than a descriptive one.

### A.9 Closing Note on the Bibliography

The bibliography covers six disciplinary areas ŌĆö affective computing theory, deep learning architecture, transfer learning methodology, synthetic data generation, edge AI deployment, and classical inferential statistics ŌĆö and is deliberately more expansive than the word-budget of any single section requires. This reflects the interdisciplinary nature of an embodied, locally-deployed, statistically-disciplined emotion recognition system: no single research tradition supplies the full complement of design decisions, and each cited work was consulted during the Phase 1 design phase and informed a specific, traceable implementation choice. Where preprint or preliminary references are cited (most notably Michelucci & Venturini, 2025), the methodological recommendations have been verified against the broader peer-reviewed literature on ML experimental design and found consistent. Full implementation traceability is available through the project's open repository at `github.com/RustyBee2016/project-reachy-emotion` on branch `claude/reachy-emotion-recognition-kE3vE` (synchronized with `codex-phase-1-stats-v4`), where the `stats/`, `trainer/`, and `memory-bank/decisions/` directories provide the code and ADR artifacts corresponding to every citation in this paper.
